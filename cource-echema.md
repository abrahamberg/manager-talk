# Structured Communication Skill Ladder

## Purpose

This framework defines the progression from basic communication to senior manager-level communication.

The goal is to train a person to move from:

> “I have ideas, but I explain them messily”

to:

> “I communicate clearly, strategically, and with leadership presence.”

This is especially useful for:

* Engineering Manager interviews
* Leadership conversations
* Stakeholder discussions
* Performance conversations
* Conflict resolution
* Strategy and prioritization discussions

***

# Core Progression

The communication skill develops in this order:

1. **Say what you did**
2. **Say what happened as a result**
3. **Start with the main point**
4. **Use structure**
5. **Use examples**
6. **Show judgment**
7. **Show tradeoffs**
8. **Show system thinking**
9. **Show strategic leadership**
10. **Communicate like a senior manager**

***

# Level 1 — Basic Clarity

## Level Name

**Action + Result**

## Goal

The trainee can explain a simple action and its result without rambling.

At this level, the trainee does not need to sound advanced.  
The only goal is to finish a thought clearly.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
I did X.
As a result, Y happened.
```

***

## What the trainee must be able to do

The trainee should be able to:

* Say what they did
* Say what changed after that
* Use short sentences
* Avoid long background
* Avoid unnecessary explanation

***

## Example Question

> “How do you handle too many tasks?”

## Good Level 1 Answer

> “I prioritize the most important tasks first.  
> As a result, I stay focused and finish the most important work.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can consistently:

* Answer in 1–2 sentences
* Clearly describe one action
* Clearly describe one result
* Avoid unrelated details
* Stop after answering

***

## Failure Pattern

Bad answer:

> “Usually there are many things happening and sometimes it depends because different people ask for different things and then I try to think about what is important…”

Why it fails:

* No clear action
* No clear result
* Too many ideas
* The listener has to guess the point

***

# Level 2 — Basic Structure

## Level Name

**Point + Action + Result**

## Goal

The trainee can start with the main idea before explaining.

This is the first real structured communication level.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The main point is X.
I do Y.
This helps with Z.
```

***

## What the trainee must be able to do

The trainee should be able to:

* Start with the point
* Explain one action
* Explain why it helps
* Keep the answer short
* Avoid discovering the answer while speaking

***

## Example Question

> “How do you make sure your team is aligned?”

## Good Level 2 Answer

> “Communication is key for alignment.  
> I make sure work is visible and documented, for example in Jira.  
> This helps the team understand priorities and stay aligned with business goals.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Start with a clear main point
* Use 3 sentences or fewer
* Explain what they do
* Explain why it matters
* Avoid vague endings

***

## Expected Vocabulary

At this level, simple words are enough:

* alignment
* priorities
* communication
* focus
* result
* clear
* visible
* documented

***

# Level 3 — Interview-Ready Structure

## Level Name

**Problem + Action + Result**

## Goal

The trainee can answer interview questions clearly using a simple story structure.

This level is enough for many basic behavioral interview questions.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The problem was X.
What I did was Y.
The result was Z.
```

***

## What the trainee must be able to do

The trainee should be able to:

* Identify the problem
* Explain their own action
* Explain the outcome
* Avoid too much background
* Avoid generic answers

***

## Example Question

> “Tell me about a time you improved ways of working.”

## Good Level 3 Answer

> “The problem was that the team was not aligned in how we worked.  
> I introduced Scrum practices to create more structure and shared communication.  
> As a result, the team became more synchronized and worked with clearer priorities.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Use a clear problem-action-result structure
* Make their own contribution obvious
* Explain what changed
* Finish the answer cleanly
* Avoid repeating the same idea several times

***

## Expected Vocabulary

* problem
* action
* result
* alignment
* ownership
* priorities
* process
* structure
* improvement

***

# Level 4 — Concrete Impact

## Level Name

**Situation + Action + Visible Change**

## Goal

The trainee can make answers believable by adding concrete evidence.

At this level, the trainee moves from abstract claims to visible impact.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The situation was X.
The issue was Y.
I did Z.
The visible change was A.
The result was B.
```

***

## What the trainee must be able to do

The trainee should be able to:

* Give a real example
* Explain what was broken or unclear
* Explain what they changed
* Describe what became different
* Make the result visible to the listener

***

## Example Question

> “How did you help your team improve?”

## Good Level 4 Answer

> “When I joined the team, our ways of working were unclear.  
> People had different expectations around priorities, planning, and ownership.  
> I introduced clearer Scrum practices, improved how we used Jira, and helped the team create a shared working rhythm.  
> The visible change was that people became more synchronized and had fewer misunderstandings about what mattered most.  
> As a result, the team became more confident and predictable in how we worked.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Give a concrete example
* Explain what changed in behavior
* Avoid only saying abstract words like “communication” or “ownership”
* Show a clear before-and-after
* Make the listener believe the story

***

## Expected Vocabulary

* working rhythm
* shared understanding
* visibility
* transparency
* predictable delivery
* clear ownership
* team maturity
* operating model
* feedback loop

***

# Level 5 — Manager-Level Communication

## Level Name

**Principle + Practice + Example + Outcome**

## Goal

The trainee can explain not only what they did, but also the leadership principle behind it.

This is where the person starts sounding like a manager, not only an individual contributor.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
My principle is X.
In practice, I do Y.
For example, Z.
The outcome is A.
```

***

## What the trainee must be able to do

The trainee should be able to:

* Explain their leadership belief
* Connect belief to practical behavior
* Give an example
* Explain the result
* Sound intentional, not accidental

***

## Example Question

> “How do you create accountability?”

## Good Level 5 Answer

> “My principle is that accountability starts with clarity.  
> In practice, I make sure people understand the goal, the expected outcome, and who owns what.  
> For example, when priorities are unclear, I help break them down into visible work and clear ownership areas.  
> This creates transparency and makes it easier for the team to follow through without micromanagement.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* State a leadership principle
* Show how they apply it
* Use an example
* Explain the outcome
* Avoid sounding like they are only doing tasks

***

## Expected Manager Vocabulary

* accountability
* clarity
* ownership
* transparency
* expected outcomes
* follow-through
* empowerment
* delegation
* alignment
* execution
* prioritization
* decision-making

***

# Level 6 — Leadership Tension

## Level Name

**Balance Two Important Things**

## Goal

The trainee can explain how they handle leadership tensions.

This is critical for Engineering Manager roles.

Managers are often not solving simple problems.  
They are balancing competing needs.

***

## Common Tensions

The trainee should be able to discuss:

* Psychological safety vs accountability
* Autonomy vs alignment
* Speed vs quality
* Structure vs flexibility
* Technical excellence vs delivery pressure
* Individual growth vs team needs
* Stakeholder expectations vs team capacity

***

## Expected Structure

```text
There are two sides to this.
One side is X.
The other side is Y.
My role is to balance them by doing Z.
The result is A.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How do you create psychological safety and accountability?”

## Good Level 6 Answer

> “For me, psychological safety and accountability need to work together.  
> Psychological safety means people feel safe to speak up, raise risks, and admit mistakes early.  
> Accountability means we still have clear ownership, expectations, and follow-through.  
> My role as a manager is to create a blame-free environment, but not an expectation-free environment.  
> In practice, I focus on learning when something goes wrong, and then I clarify next steps and ownership.  
> This helps the team be honest without avoiding responsibility.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Identify both sides of a leadership tension
* Avoid simplistic answers
* Explain their role in balancing the tension
* Give a practical behavior
* Explain the result

***

## Expected Manager Vocabulary

* psychological safety
* accountability
* ownership
* expectations
* follow-through
* autonomy
* alignment
* empowerment
* trust
* constructive feedback
* escalation
* decision clarity
* responsibility
* learning culture

# Level 7 — Senior Manager Thinking

## Level Name

**Diagnose + Adapt + Optimize**

## Goal

The trainee can explain how they diagnose a situation and adapt their leadership approach.

This is where communication starts sounding more senior.

A senior manager does not say:

> “This is the right process.”

A senior manager says:

> “It depends on the team’s maturity, context, constraints, and desired outcome.”

***

## Expected Structure

```text
First, I diagnose the context.
I look at X, Y, and Z.
Then I adapt my approach.
If the issue is A, I do B.
If the issue is C, I do D.
The goal is always E.
```

***

## Example Question

> “What is your approach to ways of working?”

## Good Level 7 Answer

> “I don’t believe one process fits every team.  
> First, I diagnose the team’s maturity, the clarity of priorities, and where the friction exists.  
> If the team lacks alignment, I introduce more structure, such as planning, clear ownership, and regular follow-up.  
> If the team is already mature and synchronized, I reduce unnecessary ceremonies and keep the process lightweight.  
> For example, in one team I introduced Scrum practices to create alignment, but later simplified the process when the team had grown into a more mature working rhythm.  
> The goal is not to follow Scrum perfectly; the goal is to create an effective operating model for the team.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Explain how they diagnose before acting
* Show that they adapt to context
* Discuss team maturity
* Avoid rigid process thinking
* Explain the leadership goal behind the action

***

## Expected Senior Manager Vocabulary

* context
* diagnosis
* team maturity
* friction
* operating model
* working rhythm
* scalability
* effectiveness
* optimization
* adaptability
* constraints
* tradeoffs
* outcome-oriented
* lightweight process
* continuous improvement

# Level 8 — Strategic Communication

## Level Name

**Connect Team Work to Business Outcomes**

## Goal

The trainee can connect team actions to business value.

This is a major difference between a team lead and a manager.

A team lead may say:

> “We improved the process.”

A manager says:

> “We improved the process so the team could deliver more predictably against business priorities.”

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The business need was X.
The team challenge was Y.
I helped the team do Z.
This improved A.
The business impact was B.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How do you make sure engineering work supports business goals?”

## Good Level 8 Answer

> “I start by making sure the team understands the business goal behind the work.  
> Then I help translate that goal into clear engineering outcomes, priorities, and tradeoffs.  
> This avoids the team simply delivering tasks without understanding why they matter.  
> In practice, I make work visible, clarify expected outcomes, and ensure we regularly check whether priorities still match business needs.  
> That helps the team stay focused on value, not only output.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Explain the business context
* Translate business goals into team outcomes
* Discuss priorities and tradeoffs
* Avoid talking only about tasks
* Show how engineering creates value

***

## Expected Strategic Vocabulary

* business outcomes
* value delivery
* strategic alignment
* prioritization
* tradeoffs
* expected outcomes
* customer value
* stakeholder alignment
* execution focus
* roadmap
* business priorities
* measurable impact
* outcome over output

# Level 9 — Executive Presence

## Level Name

**Clear, Calm, Decisive, High Signal**

## Goal

The trainee can communicate like a leader who creates confidence.

At this level, the answer should be:

* structured
* concise
* confident
* not defensive
* not overly detailed
* connected to outcomes

The person should sound like they are guiding the conversation, not surviving it.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
My view is X.
The reason is Y.
In practice, I do Z.
The impact is A.
The principle is B.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How do you lead senior engineers?”

## Good Level 9 Answer

> “My view is that senior engineers need autonomy, but they also need clear direction and strong context.  
> I don’t try to manage them through control; I manage by creating alignment, clarifying outcomes, and removing blockers.  
> Because I have a strong technical background, I can participate in technical discussions credibly, but my role is not to make every decision myself.  
> My role is to create the conditions where senior engineers can make good decisions and stay aligned with the broader strategy.  
> That balance creates trust, accountability, and high-quality execution.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Speak with confidence
* Make the main point early
* Explain their leadership philosophy
* Connect people, technology, and business
* Avoid over-explaining
* End with a strong final sentence

***

## Expected Executive Vocabulary

* autonomy
* alignment
* strategic context
* decision-making
* operating principles
* trust
* accountability
* empowerment
* execution
* technical credibility
* business impact
* leadership philosophy
* stakeholder management
* sustainable delivery

# Level 10 — Professional Senior Manager Level

## Level Name

**System Leadership + Strategic Judgment**

## Goal

The trainee can communicate like a senior manager who understands teams as systems.

At this level, the person does not only talk about individual actions.  
They talk about:

* systems
* incentives
* constraints
* organizational alignment
* leadership principles
* decision quality
* execution models
* long-term team health
* scalable ways of working

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The broader context is X.
The system challenge is Y.
My leadership approach is Z.
I create alignment through A.
I create accountability through B.
I create sustainability through C.
The expected outcome is D.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How do you build a high-performing engineering team?”

## Good Level 10 Answer

> “I see a high-performing team as a system, not just a group of strong individuals.  
> The foundation is clear direction, psychological safety, accountability, and a strong operating model.  
> First, I make sure the team understands the business context and the outcomes we are expected to achieve.  
> Then I create clarity around ownership, decision-making, and priorities, so people can act with autonomy without losing alignment.  
> I also pay attention to team health, feedback loops, and whether our ways of working are helping or slowing us down.  
> For senior engineers especially, I focus on creating context rather than control.  
> The goal is a team that can make good decisions, deliver predictably, improve continuously, and grow sustainably.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Speak about teams as systems
* Connect people, process, technology, and business
* Explain leadership principles clearly
* Show strategic judgment
* Discuss sustainability, not only short-term delivery
* Use advanced vocabulary naturally
* Sound calm, intentional, and credible

***

# Level 11 — Director-Level Communication

## Level Name

**Organization-Level Thinking**

## Goal

The trainee can communicate beyond one team.

This level is for people who can talk about:

* multiple teams
* cross-functional alignment
* organizational tradeoffs
* leadership systems
* long-term capability
* platform strategy
* talent development
* execution across teams

***

## Expected Structure

```text
At the organizational level, the challenge is X.
The risk is Y.
The leadership priority is Z.
I would address it through A, B, and C.
The success measure would be D.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How would you improve engineering effectiveness across several teams?”

## Good Level 11 Answer

> “At the organizational level, engineering effectiveness depends on clarity, flow, and decision quality.  
> The first thing I would look at is where work gets stuck: unclear priorities, dependencies, slow decisions, or lack of ownership.  
> Then I would work with managers and technical leaders to create a more consistent operating model across teams, while still allowing local autonomy.  
> I would focus on improving visibility, reducing unnecessary dependencies, and creating stronger feedback loops between engineering, product, and business stakeholders.  
> The goal would be to improve predictability, reduce friction, and increase the organization’s ability to deliver value sustainably.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Talk beyond one team
* Identify organizational bottlenecks
* Discuss cross-team operating models
* Balance standardization and autonomy
* Define success measures
* Communicate with strategic clarity

# Level 12 — Executive / VP-Level Communication

## Level Name

**Vision + Strategy + Execution System**

## Goal

The trainee can communicate like a senior executive.

At this level, the person speaks about:

* vision
* strategy
* business impact
* organizational capability
* execution at scale
* leadership culture
* risk management
* long-term competitiveness

This is beyond normal EM communication, but it is useful as the final mastery level.

***

## Expected Structure

```text
The strategic objective is X.
The current constraint is Y.
The operating model needs to enable Z.
The leadership approach is A.
The execution mechanism is B.
The success indicators are C.
The long-term impact is D.
```

***

## Example Question

> “How should engineering support business transformation?”

## Good Level 12 Answer

> “Engineering should be a strategic enabler, not only a delivery function.  
> The business objective needs to be translated into clear technical capabilities, platform investments, and execution priorities.  
> The main leadership challenge is to create an operating model where teams can move fast locally while still staying aligned globally.  
> That requires clear decision rights, strong platform foundations, effective feedback loops, and a culture of accountability.  
> I would focus on reducing organizational friction, increasing delivery predictability, and building engineering capabilities that scale beyond individual teams.  
> Success should be measured not only by output, but by business outcomes, time-to-value, reliability, and the organization’s ability to adapt.  
> The long-term impact is an engineering organization that improves speed, quality, and strategic flexibility.”

***

## Pass Criteria

The trainee passes this level when they can:

* Communicate a strategic vision
* Connect engineering to business transformation
* Discuss operating models at scale
* Define execution mechanisms
* Discuss measurable success
* Balance speed, quality, risk, and adaptability
* Sound clear, concise, and credible at executive level

# Important Coaching Rule

Advanced vocabulary should only be added after structure is stable.

A trainee should not say:

> “I optimize the operating model for strategic alignment and sustainable value delivery”

if they cannot clearly explain:

> “I helped the team work better and deliver the right things.”

The order is:

1. Clear
2. Structured
3. Concrete
4. Strategic
5. Advanced vocabulary

***

# Final Master Standard

A trainee reaches senior manager communication level when they can consistently answer like this:

> “My view is that strong engineering leadership is about creating clarity, alignment, and accountability at scale.  
> I start by understanding the business context and translating it into clear outcomes for the team.  
> Then I create an operating model where ownership, decision-making, and priorities are visible.  
> I also pay attention to psychological safety, because people need to feel safe to raise risks early, but that must be balanced with clear expectations and follow-through.  
> My goal is to build a team that can make good decisions, deliver predictably, improve continuously, and grow sustainably.”

That is the target state.

Not fancy.  
Not robotic.  
But clear, structured, strategic, and credible.
