# Level 6 Input Questions

For **Level 6**, the coach should ask questions where the trainee must handle **leadership tensions**.

This level moves from:

> “Here is my leadership principle”

to:

> “There are two important things in tension, and my job as a manager is to balance them.”

This is a key level for **Engineering Manager communication**.

***

# Level 6 Goal

The trainee should be able to explain:

1. **Two sides of a leadership tension**
2. Why both sides matter
3. What the manager does to balance them
4. What outcome this balance creates

At this level, the trainee should sound like they understand that management is rarely one-dimensional.

A weak manager says:

> “I create psychological safety.”

A stronger manager says:

> “I create psychological safety while also keeping clear accountability.”

***

# Coach Instruction for Level 6

Before each question, the coach should say:

> “Answer by explaining the two sides of the tension. Then explain how you balance them as a manager and what outcome that creates. Keep it to 5–7 sentences.”

The coach should push the trainee to avoid one-sided answers.

The trainee should not only say:

> “I give people autonomy.”

They should say:

> “I give autonomy, but I also create alignment around goals, constraints, and expected outcomes.”

***

# Expected Level 6 Structure

```text
There are two sides to this.
One side is X.
The other side is Y.
Both are important because Z.
As a manager, I balance them by doing A.
For example, B.
The result is C.
```

Or more naturally:

```text
For me, X and Y need to work together.
X means...
Y means...
My role is to balance them by...
In practice, that means...
This creates...
```

# Expected Level 6 Answer Pattern

The coach should expect answers like:

```text
For me, psychological safety and accountability need to work together.
Psychological safety means people feel safe to speak up, raise risks, and admit mistakes early.
Accountability means we still have clear ownership, expectations, and follow-through.
My role as a manager is to create a blame-free environment, but not an expectation-free environment.
In practice, I focus on learning first when something goes wrong, and then I clarify ownership for the next steps.
This helps the team be honest without avoiding responsibility.
```

Or:

```text
Autonomy and alignment need to work together.
Autonomy means engineers have space to make decisions and solve problems.
Alignment means they understand the goals, constraints, and priorities.
As a manager, I provide context and clear outcomes, but I avoid controlling every implementation detail.
For example, I define what success looks like and let the team decide how to get there.
This creates ownership without people moving in different directions.
```

# Example Level 6 Answers

## Question

> How do you create psychological safety and accountability?

Good answer:

> “For me, psychological safety and accountability need to work together.  
> Psychological safety means people feel safe to speak up, raise concerns, and admit mistakes early.  
> Accountability means we still have clear ownership, expectations, and follow-through.  
> My role as a manager is to create a blame-free environment, but not an expectation-free environment.  
> In practice, when something goes wrong, I focus first on learning, then I clarify what we will do next and who owns it.  
> This helps the team be honest while still taking responsibility.”

***

## Question

> How do you give autonomy while keeping alignment?

Good answer:

> “Autonomy and alignment need to work together.  
> Autonomy means people have space to make decisions and use their expertise.  
> Alignment means they understand the business goal, priorities, and constraints.  
> As a manager, I create context instead of control.  
> In practice, I clarify the expected outcome and decision boundaries, then let the team decide the best solution.  
> This helps people take ownership without drifting away from the broader goal.”

***

## Question

> How do you balance speed and quality?

Good answer:

> “Speed and quality are always in tension, especially when delivery pressure is high.  
> Speed matters because the business needs value quickly.  
> Quality matters because poor technical decisions create future cost and reduce delivery speed later.  
> My role is to make the tradeoff explicit instead of pretending there is no tradeoff.  
> In practice, I ask what risk we are accepting, what can be simplified safely, and what must not be compromised.  
> This helps the team move fast without creating hidden technical debt.”

***

## Question

> How do you balance structure and flexibility?

Good answer:

> “Structure and flexibility both matter in ways of working.  
> Structure gives the team clarity, rhythm, and shared expectations.  
> Flexibility allows the team to adapt when priorities change or when the process no longer adds value.  
> As a manager, I adjust the process based on team maturity and friction.  
> For example, if the team lacks alignment, I add more planning and visibility; if the team is mature, I remove unnecessary ceremonies.  
> This keeps the operating model useful instead of bureaucratic.”

***

## Question

> How do you balance technical leadership and people management?

Good answer:

> “Technical credibility and people leadership both matter for an Engineering Manager.  
> Technical credibility helps me understand tradeoffs and build trust with senior engineers.  
> People leadership ensures I am not becoming the bottleneck or making every decision myself.  
> My role is to create context, ask good questions, and help the team make strong decisions.  
> I step into technical discussions when risk, alignment, or architecture direction needs support, but I step back when the team can own the decision.  
> This gives the team autonomy while still benefiting from my technical background.”

***

## Question

> How do you balance stakeholder expectations and team capacity?

Good answer:

> “Stakeholder expectations and team capacity need to be balanced transparently.  
> Stakeholders need clarity on what can be delivered and when.  
> The team needs focus and sustainable workload to deliver well.  
> My role is to make capacity and tradeoffs visible.  
> In practice, I clarify priorities, explain impact, and negotiate scope when there is more demand than capacity.  
> This helps the business make informed decisions without overloading the team.”

***

# Level 6 Pass Criteria

The trainee passes one answer if:

* It is **5–7 sentences**
* It clearly identifies **two sides of the tension**
* It explains why both sides matter
* It explains the manager’s role in balancing them
* It gives at least one practical behavior
* It explains the outcome
* It avoids one-sided or simplistic answers
* It sounds like leadership judgment, not just task execution

***

# Level 6 Scoring

The coach can score each answer out of 12.

## 1. Tension Identified

* 0 = no tension identified
* 1 = only one side identified
* 2 = both sides identified clearly

## 2. Why Both Matter

* 0 = no explanation
* 1 = weak explanation
* 2 = clear explanation

## 3. Manager Role

* 0 = no manager role
* 1 = vague manager role
* 2 = clear manager role

## 4. Practical Behavior

* 0 = no practical behavior
* 1 = vague behavior
* 2 = clear behavior

## 5. Outcome

* 0 = no outcome
* 1 = weak outcome
* 2 = clear outcome

## 6. Leadership Maturity

* 0 = simplistic or one-sided
* 1 = somewhat mature
* 2 = strong leadership judgment

## Passing Score

The trainee passes the answer with:

```text
10 out of 12 or higher
```

# Common Level 6 Mistakes

## Mistake 1 — Only answering one side

Weak answer:

> “I create psychological safety by listening to people and letting them speak.”

Why it fails:

* Only covers safety
* Does not cover accountability
* Does not show leadership tension

Better:

> “Psychological safety means people can speak up. Accountability means expectations and ownership are still clear. My role is to create both.”

***

## Mistake 2 — Using buzzwords without explaining them

Weak answer:

> “It is about autonomy, alignment, empowerment, and accountability.”

Why it fails:

* Abstract
* No practical behavior
* No balance

Better:

> “Autonomy means people choose how to solve problems. Alignment means they understand the goal and constraints. I balance them by defining outcomes clearly and letting the team choose the implementation.”

***

## Mistake 3 — Sounding too controlling

Weak answer:

> “I give autonomy, but I check everything to make sure people are doing it correctly.”

Why it fails:

* Sounds like micromanagement
* Weak trust signal

Better:

> “I create autonomy by agreeing on outcomes and checkpoints, not by controlling every step.”

***

## Mistake 4 — Sounding too passive

Weak answer:

> “I trust people and let them decide.”

Why it fails:

* No alignment
* No accountability
* No manager contribution

Better:

> “I trust people to decide, but I make sure the context, priorities, and decision boundaries are clear.”

***

## Mistake 5 — No tradeoff

Weak answer:

> “We should always focus on quality.”

Why it fails:

* Ignores business urgency
* Sounds rigid

Better:

> “Quality matters, but the right level of quality depends on risk, reversibility, and business urgency.”

***

# Coach Correction Script

If the trainee gives a weak Level 6 answer, the coach should say:

```text
Good idea. Now make it Level 6.

Sentence 1: What are the two sides of the tension?
Sentence 2: Why does side one matter?
Sentence 3: Why does side two matter?
Sentence 4: What is your role as manager?
Sentence 5: What do you do in practice?
Sentence 6: What outcome does this create?
```

If the trainee struggles, ask:

```text
What are you trying to balance?
What happens if you only focus on one side?
What happens if you ignore the other side?
What do you do as a manager to keep both?
What result does that create?
```

Then ask the trainee to repeat the full answer.

***

# Coach Push Questions for Level 6

Use these when the answer is too simple:

```text
What is the tension here?
What are the two things you need to balance?
Why does each side matter?
What happens if you over-index on one side?
What do you do to balance them?
How do you make the tradeoff explicit?
What does this look like in practice?
How do you avoid micromanagement?
How do you avoid being too passive?
What outcome does this balance create?
```

# Level 6 Completion Standard

The trainee is ready to move to Level 7 when they can answer **10 different Level 6 questions** and pass at least **8 out of 10** without coach support.

They should consistently be able to answer in this form:

```text
There are two sides to this.
One side is X.
The other side is Y.
My role is to balance them by doing Z.
The result is A.
```

At this level, the trainee should sound:

> **balanced, mature, and manager-ready.**
