# Level 7 Input Questions

For **Level 7**, the coach should ask questions where the trainee must show **diagnosis + adaptation**.

This level moves from:

> “I balance two things”

to:

> “I first understand the context, then I choose the right leadership approach.”

This is where the trainee starts sounding like a **senior Engineering Manager**, not just a new manager.

***

# Level 7 Goal

The trainee should be able to explain:

1. **How they diagnose the situation**
2. **What signals they look for**
3. **How they adapt their approach**
4. **Why that approach fits the context**
5. **What outcome they are trying to create**

At this level, the trainee should avoid one-size-fits-all answers.

A weaker answer says:

> “I use Scrum to improve ways of working.”

A stronger answer says:

> “First, I look at team maturity, alignment, delivery friction, and type of work. If the team lacks structure, I add process. If the team is mature, I keep the process lightweight.”

***

# Coach Instruction for Level 7

Before each question, the coach should say:

> “Answer by explaining how you diagnose the situation first. Then explain how you adapt your approach based on what you find. Keep it to 6–8 sentences.”

The coach should push the trainee to avoid fixed answers like:

> “I always do X.”

Instead, the trainee should say:

> “It depends on the situation. I first look at X, Y, and Z. Based on that, I choose the right approach.”

***

# Expected Level 7 Structure

```text
I don’t believe one approach fits every situation.
First, I diagnose X, Y, and Z.
If the issue is A, I do B.
If the issue is C, I do D.
The reason is E.
The outcome I want is F.
```

Or more naturally:

```text
My approach depends on the context.
I first look at...
Based on that, I adapt...
For example, if...
But if...
The goal is...
```

# Level 7 Input Questions

2. How do you know if a team needs more structure?
3. How do you know if a team has too much process?
4. How do you evolve a team’s operating model over time?
5. How do you decide whether Scrum, Kanban, or a lightweight process is better?
6. How do you adapt ways of working for a platform team?
7. How do you handle a team that has many interruptions?
8. How do you improve delivery predictability?
9. How do you identify friction in the team’s workflow?
10. How do you know when a process is helping or slowing the team down?

3. How do you help a team grow from needing structure to being self-organized?
4. How do you know when a team is ready for more autonomy?
5. How do you handle a team that lacks ownership?
6. How do you handle a team that is technically strong but not aligned?
7. How do you lead a senior team differently from a junior team?
8. How do you support a team that has low confidence?

4. How do you manage someone who wants a lot of autonomy?
5. How do you adapt your leadership style for senior engineers?
6. How do you adapt your leadership style for someone underperforming?

9. How do you handle different motivations in the same team?
10. How do you know when to step in and when to step back?

4. How do you adapt decision-making based on urgency?

## Category 6 — Stakeholder and Business Context

## Category 7 — Technical Leadership Adaptation

1. How do you decide when to get involved in technical discussions?

# Expected Level 7 Answer Pattern

The coach should expect answers like:

```text
I don’t believe one way of working fits every team.
First, I look at the team’s maturity, clarity of priorities, type of work, and where the friction is.
If the team lacks alignment, I add more structure through planning, visibility, and clearer ownership.
If the team is already mature, I keep the process lightweight and remove ceremonies that no longer add value.
For example, with a platform team, I would also look at interruptions and support work before deciding on Scrum or Kanban.
The goal is to create an operating model that improves flow, alignment, and predictability without adding unnecessary bureaucracy.
```

Or:

```text
My leadership style depends on the person and the situation.
First, I look at the person’s skill level, confidence, motivation, and how much context they have.
If someone is capable but lacks confidence, I coach and support them more closely.
If someone is senior and independent, I focus more on context, alignment, and removing blockers.
The goal is to give each person the right level of support and autonomy.
That helps people grow without creating dependency on me.
```

# Example Level 7 Answers

## Question

> How do you decide what way of working a team needs?

Good answer:

> “I don’t believe one process fits every team.  
> First, I diagnose the team’s maturity, clarity of priorities, type of work, and delivery friction.  
> If the team lacks alignment, I introduce more structure, such as planning, visible ownership, and regular follow-up.  
> If the team is already synchronized and mature, I reduce unnecessary ceremonies and keep the process lightweight.  
> For platform teams, I also look at interruptions, support requests, and dependency flow before deciding on the right model.  
> The goal is to create an operating model that helps the team deliver predictably without adding bureaucracy.”

***

## Question

> How do you assess team maturity?

Good answer:

> “I assess team maturity by looking at ownership, decision-making, collaboration, and delivery predictability.  
> A mature team does not only have strong engineers; it also has shared understanding and good follow-through.  
> If the team waits for direction, I know they may need more structure and clearer expectations.  
> If the team raises risks early, makes decisions, and improves its own way of working, that signals higher maturity.  
> Based on that, I adapt my leadership style by either adding support or giving more autonomy.  
> The goal is to help the team become more self-sufficient over time.”

***

## Question

> How do you decide when to step into technical details?

Good answer:

> “I don’t want to become the technical bottleneck, so I choose carefully when to step in.  
> I get involved when there is high risk, unclear architecture direction, strong disagreement, or impact outside the team.  
> If the decision is local and the team has enough context, I step back and let the engineers own it.  
> When I do step in, I try to ask questions and clarify tradeoffs rather than dictate the solution.  
> This keeps technical quality high while still giving the team ownership.  
> The goal is to use my technical background as leverage, not as control.”

***

## Question

> How do you diagnose why a team is not delivering?

Good answer:

> “I first try to understand whether the issue is capacity, clarity, capability, or dependencies.  
> If the team has too much work, the problem is capacity and prioritization.  
> If people are busy but outcomes are unclear, the problem is clarity.  
> If the team lacks skills or confidence, the problem may be capability.  
> If work is blocked outside the team, the issue is dependency management.  
> Once I understand the real cause, I can choose the right action instead of applying a generic fix.”

***

## Question

> How do you adapt your management style to different people?

Good answer:

> “I adapt my management style based on the person’s experience, confidence, motivation, and current challenge.  
> Some people need more coaching and frequent check-ins because the work is new for them.  
> Others need more autonomy and only need context, priorities, and decision boundaries.  
> I try to understand what each person needs to succeed rather than managing everyone the same way.  
> This helps people feel supported without feeling controlled.  
> The goal is to help each person grow while still keeping the team aligned.”

***

# Level 7 Pass Criteria

The trainee passes one answer if:

* It is **6–8 sentences**
* It starts with context or diagnosis
* It identifies at least **2–4 signals** they look for
* It explains how they adapt their approach
* It avoids “always” / one-size-fits-all thinking
* It explains why the approach fits the situation
* It ends with a clear intended outcome
* It sounds like judgment, not just process knowledge

***

# Level 7 Scoring

The coach can score each answer out of 12.

## 1. Diagnosis

* 0 = no diagnosis
* 1 = vague diagnosis
* 2 = clear diagnosis

## 2. Signals

* 0 = no signals
* 1 = 1 signal only
* 2 = 2 or more clear signals

## 3. Adaptation

* 0 = no adaptation
* 1 = weak adaptation
* 2 = clear adaptation based on context

## 4. Judgment

* 0 = rigid / one-size-fits-all
* 1 = some judgment
* 2 = strong situational judgment

## 5. Practicality

* 0 = theoretical only
* 1 = somewhat practical
* 2 = practical actions included

## 6. Outcome

* 0 = no outcome
* 1 = weak outcome
* 2 = clear outcome

## Passing Score

The trainee passes the answer with:

```text
10 out of 12 or higher
```

# Common Level 7 Mistakes

## Mistake 1 — Giving a fixed answer

Weak answer:

> “I use Scrum because it helps the team work better.”

Why it fails:

* Too rigid
* No diagnosis
* No context
* Sounds junior

Better:

> “I first look at the team’s maturity, type of work, and where the friction is. If the team lacks alignment, Scrum practices can help. If the team has many interrupts, a Kanban-style flow may work better.”

***

## Mistake 2 — Too many buzzwords, no diagnosis

Weak answer:

> “I optimize the operating model to improve flow, alignment, and delivery predictability.”

Why it fails:

* Sounds advanced but empty
* No evidence of how they diagnose
* No concrete action

Better:

> “I look at where work gets stuck: unclear priorities, too much WIP, dependencies, or slow decisions. Then I adjust the operating model based on that.”

***

## Mistake 3 — No adaptation

Weak answer:

> “I communicate more and clarify priorities.”

Why it fails:

* Could be Level 2–3
* Does not show senior thinking

Better:

> “If the issue is unclear priorities, I clarify outcomes. If the issue is too many dependencies, I focus on coordination and escalation. If the issue is low ownership, I clarify decision rights and responsibilities.”

***

## Mistake 4 — No signals

Weak answer:

> “I know the team is mature when they work well.”

Why it fails:

* “Work well” is too vague

Better:

> “I look for signs like raising risks early, making decisions without waiting for permission, following through on ownership, and improving their own process.”

***

## Mistake 5 — Over-explaining

Weak answer:

> “There are many different situations, and sometimes it depends on the person, and sometimes on the team, and if the team is this or that…”

Why it fails:

* Too loose
* No clean structure

Better:

> “I look at three things: maturity, clarity, and friction. Based on those, I decide whether to add structure, remove process, or focus on alignment.”

***

# Coach Correction Script

If the trainee gives a weak Level 7 answer, the coach should say:

```text
Good idea. Now make it Level 7.

Sentence 1: Say that your approach depends on context.
Sentence 2: What do you diagnose first?
Sentence 3: What signals do you look for?
Sentence 4: If the issue is A, what do you do?
Sentence 5: If the issue is B, what do you do?
Sentence 6: What outcome are you trying to create?
```

If the trainee struggles, ask:

```text
What do you check before choosing an approach?
What signals tell you what the real problem is?
What would you do if the team needs more structure?
What would you do if the team is already mature?
How do you adapt your role?
What is the goal of your approach?
```

Then ask the trainee to repeat the full answer.

***

# Coach Push Questions for Level 7

# Level 7 Completion Standard

The trainee is ready to move to Level 8 when they can answer **10 different Level 7 questions** and pass at least **8 out of 10** without coach support.

They should consistently be able to answer in this form:

```text
My approach depends on context.
First, I diagnose X, Y, and Z.
If the issue is A, I do B.
If the issue is C, I do D.
The goal is E.
```

At this level, the trainee should sound:

> **situational, adaptive, and senior-manager-like.**
